Learn about items

Not sure which bin your item belongs in? Browse the 10 categories below to learn how to recycle, which container to use, and why it matters.

🧴

Plastic

Plastic bottle
Plastic box
Yellow Container For plastic packaging, bottles, trays, and films.

How to recycle

  • Empty and rinse containers — no food residue.
  • Remove caps and lids (recycle separately or keep on bottle).
  • Flatten bottles to save space in the bin.
  • Check the recycling symbol (♳–♷) on the bottom.

Why recycle

Recycling one plastic bottle saves enough energy to power a 60 W light bulb for 3 hours. Plastic takes up to 500 years to decompose in a landfill, releasing toxic chemicals into soil and water.

🍾

Glass

Glass bottle
Glass jar
Green Container For glass bottles, jars, and food-grade glassware.

How to recycle

  • Rinse the bottle or jar — remove all food residue.
  • Remove metal lids and caps (put them in the metal bin).
  • Do NOT include broken glass, mirrors, or ceramics.
  • Separate by colour if your area requires it (clear / green / brown).

Why recycle

Glass is 100% recyclable and can be recycled endlessly without any loss of quality. Recycling one glass bottle saves enough energy to power a laptop for 25 minutes and reduces CO₂ emissions by 315 g.

📰

Paper

Paper sheets
Newspaper
Blue Container For newspapers, office paper, magazines, and envelopes.

How to recycle

  • Keep paper dry — wet or greasy paper cannot be recycled.
  • Remove staples, plastic windows from envelopes, and tape.
  • Do NOT include tissues, paper towels, or waxed paper.
  • Stack or bundle loosely — no need to shred.

Why recycle

Recycling 1 tonne of paper saves 17 trees, 26,500 litres of water, and 4,100 kWh of energy. Every tonne of recycled paper also keeps 3 m³ of landfill space free.

📦

Cardboard & Books

Cardboard box
Book
Blue Container For cardboard boxes, cartons, and paper-based books.

How to recycle

  • Flatten all cardboard boxes to maximise bin space.
  • Remove any plastic tape, foam padding, or polystyrene inserts.
  • For books: hardcovers may need the cover removed (glue & board).
  • Pizza boxes are OK only if the bottom is not soaked in grease.

Why recycle

Cardboard is one of the most widely recycled materials — up to 80% of corrugated cardboard is recovered globally. Recycling it cuts the energy needed to produce new cardboard by 75% and drastically reduces deforestation.

🥫

Metal & Cans

Tin can
Orange Container For steel and aluminium cans, foil trays, and tins.

How to recycle

  • Rinse cans to remove food — no need to scrub perfectly.
  • Crush cans to save space (where allowed).
  • Aluminium foil: scrunch into a ball before placing in the bin.
  • Do NOT include paint tins, aerosols, or pressurised canisters.

Why recycle

Aluminium can be recycled forever. Recycling one aluminium can saves enough energy to run a TV for 3 hours and produces 95% less CO₂ than making new aluminium from raw ore.

Electronics

Laptop
Mobile phone
Light bulb
Red Container / E-Waste Drop-off For electronics — take to certified e-waste collection points.

How to recycle

  • Delete personal data and perform a factory reset before dropping off.
  • Take to a certified e-waste collection point or electronics retailer.
  • Do NOT place in regular household bins — toxic materials inside.
  • Keep all components together (charger, cables, accessories).

Why recycle

E-waste is the fastest-growing waste stream globally. A single smartphone contains gold, silver, copper, and rare earth metals. Recycling 1 million phones recovers ~35 kg of gold, 350 kg of silver, and 15,000 kg of copper.

🔋

Batteries

Batteries
Red Container / Hazardous For all battery types — dedicated battery collection boxes.

How to recycle

  • Tape the terminals of lithium batteries with clear tape to prevent sparks.
  • Collect in a small container until you have enough to drop off.
  • Most supermarkets and electronics stores have free collection boxes.
  • Never puncture, crush, or throw batteries into fire.

Why recycle

Batteries contain lead, mercury, cadmium, and lithium — all harmful to soil and groundwater. Properly recycling batteries recovers valuable materials and prevents toxic leachate from contaminating ecosystems for decades.

Clothing & Textiles

Shirt
Sweater
Jeans
Shoes
Textile
Brown Container / Textile Bank Textile donation banks or charity collection points.

How to recycle

  • Wash and dry clothes before donating — clean items only.
  • Wearable clothes → charity shops or clothing banks.
  • Worn-out items → textile recycling banks (they get shredded into rags or insulation).
  • Tie shoes together so they are not separated.

Why recycle

The fashion industry is the world's 2nd largest polluter. Recycling 1 kg of cotton saves 10,000 litres of water. Keeping clothes out of landfill reduces methane emissions and prevents synthetic fibres from entering waterways.

Organic & Food Waste

🥦 Vegetable scraps
☕ Coffee grounds
🍌 Fruit peels
Brown Container For food scraps, fruit & vegetable peels, and garden waste.

How to recycle

  • Use a small kitchen caddy lined with a compostable bag.
  • Include: fruit & vegetable peelings, tea bags, eggshells, cooked food.
  • Do NOT include: liquids, oils, packaging, or pet waste.
  • Empty the caddy regularly to prevent odours and pests.

Why recycle

Organic waste in landfills produces methane — a greenhouse gas 25× more potent than CO₂. Composting instead turns waste into nutrient-rich soil improver, reducing the need for chemical fertilisers.

🪵

Wood & Furniture

Wooden stool
Black Container / Waste Centre Take large wooden items to a local recycling / civic amenity centre.

How to recycle

  • Small untreated wood scraps can go to a garden composter.
  • Large items: take to a civic amenity / waste centre wood skip.
  • Treated or painted wood must go to specialist collection — not garden compost.
  • Good-condition furniture? Donate to charity or a furniture reuse scheme first.

Why recycle

Wood makes up a significant portion of bulky waste. Recycled wood is chipped into biomass fuel, animal bedding, or particleboard. This diverts material from landfill and reduces the carbon locked in the wood from being released as methane.

✏️

Stationery

Pen
Pencil
Blue Container / Special Scheme Wooden pencils → blue bin. Plastic pens → TerraCycle drop-off point.

How to recycle

  • Wooden pencils (no metal ferrule): can go in the blue paper/cardboard bin.
  • Ballpoint & felt-tip pens: collect and drop off at a TerraCycle Writing Instruments scheme (available at many office-supply shops).
  • Refillable pens: buy refill cartridges instead — no recycling needed.
  • Rubber erasers: currently not recyclable — go in general waste.

Why recycle

Billions of pens are thrown away every year worldwide, contributing to plastic pollution. Recycling through dedicated schemes recovers the plastic barrel, metal clip, and ink cartridge separately, keeping all three out of landfill.

Garden Waste

🍃 Leaves
🌱 Plant trimmings
🌾 Grass clippings
Green Container / Garden Bin For leaves, grass clippings, hedge trimmings, and plant cuttings.

How to recycle

  • Place in the green garden waste bin or home compost heap.
  • Do NOT include soil, stones, treated wood, or plastic pots.
  • Larger branches: chop into pieces under 30 cm or take to a civic amenity site.
  • Grass clippings: leave them on the lawn as a natural fertiliser (grasscycling).

Why recycle

Garden waste makes up around 20% of household waste. When composted, it becomes a valuable soil conditioner that improves soil structure, retains moisture, and reduces the need for synthetic fertilisers — closing the nutrient loop naturally.
